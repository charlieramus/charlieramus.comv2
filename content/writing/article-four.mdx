I keep my entire life in a git repo. Daily markdown files, a personal dashboard with net worth tracking, cash flow forecasting and Sankey diagrams that show me exactly where every dollar leaks out of my month. I am probably the most optimized seventeen year old you will ever meet. I tell you this up front so that when I start arguing against optimization, you understand I am not pointing at someone else. I am the disease describing its own symptoms.

So here is the thing I keep circling back to. Somewhere out there is a video, and you have probably seen a version of it, that says a happy life needs six hobbies. One to keep your body alive. One that anchors you to other people. One that feeds your mind. One where you make something instead of just consuming. One for your inner life, the quiet processing. And one with real stakes, something that matters and isn't assigned to you. Six faces. A clean little hexagon you can tape to your wall.

I watched it. I nodded. And then, like an idiot, I started sorting myself into the boxes. Climbing covers body and belonging. Reading covers mind. The side projects cover craft and stakes both. Journaling covers the inner one. Look at me go. Six for six. Balanced. Optimized. Flourishing on schedule.

You are doing it right now. You read that list and something in you started taking inventory. Body, sure, fine. Belonging, maybe a small flinch. Inner life, a bigger flinch. You can feel exactly which face has gone dark on you. That flinch is the whole essay. You just took six different ways of being alive and turned them into a report card you are quietly failing.

Because here is what nobody says about the hexagon. The moment you can explain why a hobby is good for you, you have already started to kill it. The second you instrument play, measure it, slot it into a framework that earns its keep, it stops being play. It becomes another input. Another box with a green check or a red one. You took the last corner of your life that was supposed to exist for no reason at all and you gave it a reason. Congratulations. You turned your weekend into a job you forgot to bill for.

My favorite game is one where you crash land on an alien planet and build factories. You lay down conveyor belts that grow into these enormous elevated highways of moving resources, splitting and merging and looping back on themselves, and to do it well you genuinely have to do math. Throughput per minute. Production ratios. People build spreadsheets and flowcharts just to plan a single factory floor. It is, by any honest description, unpaid industrial engineering. And I love it. I love it precisely because nobody is paying me, nothing depends on it and no one will ever audit my belt efficiency. The instant someone handed me a deadline and a paycheck for it, the joy would drain out by the end of the afternoon.

In my opinion the hexagon is useful exactly once, and only if you hold it the way you'd hold a thermometer instead of a scoreboard. You glance at it when something feels off, notice which face has gone cold and then put it away and forget the whole geometry. That is the only honest use. The moment it graduates from a thing you occasionally check into a thing you check daily, it quietly stops being a map of a good life and becomes the bars of a very tasteful cage. Same shape. Same six sides. Except now you are inside it, ranking your own joy and calling that wisdom.

I still have the dashboard. I still have the markdown files. I still have some rough hexagon floating in the back of my head that I never asked to be there, and I know it is a little arbitrary and a little dangerous and probably making my own hobbies slightly worse just by existing. I have not figured out how to want less than that. Which is the only reason I didn't try to fix yours.
